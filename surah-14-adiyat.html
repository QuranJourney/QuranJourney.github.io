Surah Al-Adiyat

The Charging Steeds • Makkah • 11 Verses
The Anatomy of Ingratitude. Allah uses the breathtaking imagery of loyal war horses plunging into danger for their master to highlight the tragic paradox of human beings: we are given everything by God, yet we remain fiercely attached to our wealth and deeply ungrateful.
Verse 1
وَٱلْعَـٰدِيَـٰتِ ضَبْحًا
"By the racers, panting,"
Plain Understanding
An oath sworn by the charging war horses, panting heavily and snorting as they sprint into battle.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
The Arabs valued horses above all other possessions for their bravery and loyalty. "Dabh" is the specific sound of the horse's breath catching in its chest as it exerts maximum physical effort for its rider.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
The horse submits entirely to its master, plunging into mortal danger without questioning the command. The soul should have this level of absolute submission to the Divine Command, charging against the ego (Jihad al-Nafs) until it pants from the effort.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
Look at the sheer loyalty of the animal to its human master, despite the master only providing it a few handfuls of oats. How much more loyal should the human be to the Divine Master who provides absolute existence?

Verse 2
فَٱلْمُورِيَـٰتِ قَدْحًا
"And the producers of sparks [when] striking"
Plain Understanding
Swearing by the horses whose hooves strike the rocky ground so forcefully they ignite sparks of fire.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
This imagery sets the scene of a night or pre-dawn raid. The velocity and raw power of the iron horseshoes hitting the desert stones light up the darkness.
Purification of the Self (Al-Jilani)
The friction of spiritual discipline (Mujahada) creates the sparks of divine light (Nur) in the heart. Without the friction of struggling against your base desires, the fire of divine love cannot be ignited.

Verse 3
فَٱلْمُغِيرَٰتِ صُبْحًا
"And the chargers at dawn,"
Plain Understanding
Those steeds that take the enemy by complete surprise with a sudden, overwhelming raid at the break of dawn.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
Arabs typically attacked at dawn. It represents the perfect execution of strategy, combining the cover of darkness with the light needed to strike.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
The dawn (Fajr) is the time of spiritual victory. The true believer raids their own heedlessness exactly at dawn, capturing their heart for Allah before the distractions of the world can capture it.
Knowledge & SignsSpiritual Practice
Verse 4
فَأَثَرْنَ بِهِۦ نَقْعًا
"Stirring up thereby [clouds of] dust,"
Plain Understanding
The massive charge kicks up thick, blinding clouds of dust into the air.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
The sheer numbers, speed, and intensity of the horses overwhelm the environment, blinding the enemy with the dust of their arrival.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
When you finally charge toward Allah with full sincerity, the "dust" of your past attachments, bad habits, and people's worldly opinions will be kicked up around you. Ignore the dust; keep your eyes fixed on the destination.

Verse 5
فَوَسَطْنَ بِهِۦ جَمْعًا
"Arriving thereby in the center collectively,"
Plain Understanding
Piercing straight into the very heart and center of the enemy ranks as one unified force.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
The horses don't hesitate or linger on the safe edges of the battlefield; they plunge fearlessly right into the middle of the danger, completely trusting their rider.
Purification of the Self (Al-Jilani)
The goal is the center (the Divine Presence). Do not linger on the outer edges of religion (mere outward rituals and debates); plunge straight into the heart of Ihsan (spiritual excellence and presence).

Verse 6
إِنَّ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنَ لِرَبِّهِۦ لَكَنُودٌ
"Indeed mankind, to his Lord, is ungrateful."
Plain Understanding
This is what the oaths were building up to: To prove that human beings are intensely ungrateful, stubborn, and unyielding to their Creator.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
"Kanud" is a specific, severe type of ingratitude: it describes someone who meticulously counts the calamities that befall him, but completely forgets the countless blessings he receives. The contrast is devastating: the horse runs into death for its master, but the human refuses to even pray to his Master.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
The ego is fundamentally Kanud. It focuses on the one time God said "no" and ignores the million times God said "yes." Spiritual maturity begins with reversing this metric—becoming intensely aware of the blessings and patient with the tests.

Verse 7
وَإِنَّهُۥ عَلَىٰ ذَٰلِكَ لَشَهِيدٌ
"And indeed, he is to that a witness."
Plain Understanding
And the human being knows this. Deep down, he is a living witness against his own ingratitude.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
You do not need an external judge to prove your ingratitude. Your own conscience, your own actions, and your refusal to give charity all stand as direct testimonies against you.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
"Your witnessing of His blessings upon you is what demands your gratitude." But here, the human witnesses their own failure. The very first step of repentance (Tawbah) is to stop making excuses and witness your own "Kanud" (ingratitude).

Verse 8
وَإِنَّهُۥ لِحُبِّ ٱلْخَيْرِ لَشَهِيدٌ
"And indeed he is, in love of wealth, intense."
Plain Understanding
And truly, the human being has a severe, violent, and passionate love for wealth and material goods.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
The Quran uses the word "Khayr" (goodness) ironically here to mean "wealth," because humans perceive money as the ultimate good. This intense love of wealth is the exact root cause of the ingratitude mentioned in Verse 6.
Purification of the Self (Al-Jilani)
Wealth itself is not the disease; the "intense love" (Hubb Shadid) of wealth is the disease. When the heart loves the provision more than the Provider, it becomes a slave to the gold coin, leading to spiritual death.

Verse 9
۞ أَفَلَا يَعْلَمُ إِذَا بُعْثِرَ مَا فِى ٱلْقُبُورِ
"But does he not know that when the contents of the graves are scattered"
Plain Understanding
Does this greedy, ungrateful person not realize what will happen when the graves are violently turned inside out and the dead are resurrected?
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
"Bu'thira" means to rummage, scatter, and forcefully pull out what is hidden inside. The graves will be emptied. Death is not a permanent resting place; it is merely a waiting room for accountability.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
"Travel from the creation to the Creator." The obsession with wealth ends the exact moment the dirt of the grave is scattered. Why invest so intensely in a world you are about to be violently pulled out of?

Verse 10
وَحُصِّلَ مَا فِى ٱلصُّدُورِ
"And that within the breasts is obtained,"
Plain Understanding
And all the secrets, intentions, and hidden motives locked inside the chests of men are extracted and exposed.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
"Hussila" means to extract the core from the shell, like extracting the kernel of a nut. It's not just your physical actions that are judged, but the deep, hidden motives behind them.
Purification of the Self (Al-Ghazali)
In the Dunya, you are judged by your outward actions. People see you pray or give charity and call you righteous. On the Day of Judgment, you are judged by the secrets of your heart. If your heart was full of Riya (showing off) or the "love of wealth" (verse 8), it will be disastrously exposed here.

Verse 11
إِنَّ رَبَّهُم بِهِمْ يَوْمَئِذٍ لَّخَبِيرٌۢ
"Indeed, their Lord with them, that Day, is [fully] Acquainted."
Plain Understanding
Truly, their Master on that Day is completely, intimately aware of everything they did and exactly why they did it.
Historical Context (Al-Qurtubi)
"Khabir" means the one who knows the deepest, most subtle inner workings of a thing. God has always been Khabir, but on "that Day," His intimate knowledge will manifest as absolute, undeniable Justice.
Divine Wisdom (Ibn Ata’illah)
"He knew your faults before He created you, and His mercy encompassed you." The realization that He is completely Acquainted (Khabir) with your darkest secrets should make you humble, but not hopeless—for He is the Lord of Mercy who loves to forgive the one who repents.
